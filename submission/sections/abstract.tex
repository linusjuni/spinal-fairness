\begin{abstract}
Automated cervical-spine MRI segmentation increasingly informs stenosis grading and
the tracking of degenerative change, yet no demographic fairness audit exists for this
anatomy. Auditing it is complicated by a property of modern segmentation datasets: to
contain annotation cost they mix scarce expert (gold) labels with abundant
machine-generated (silver) ones, so the reference used to judge a model can itself be
flawed -- the biased-ruler effect introduced for segmentation by Parikh et al. We
present the first fairness audit of cervical-spine MRI segmentation, training nnU-Net
models on CSpineSeg ($1{,}254$ exams) and evaluating across sex, race, and age. The
deployed model is fair by every standard metric. The choice of reference label,
however, is not neutral. Because a dataset's silver labels are generated by a model
trained on its gold labels, any new model trained on those same gold labels agrees more
with the silver labels than with expert truth: scoring identical predictions against
silver rather than gold overestimates performance by ${\sim}8$ Dice points and turns
the fairness verdict for age from non-significant to significant -- not by inflating the
gap (Parikh et al.'s \emph{false magnitude}) but by collapsing within-group variance
(\emph{false confidence}). Reference-label provenance
is thus a first-order confounder in segmentation evaluation: performance and fairness
should be reported against expert labels, and any fairness claim stated together with
the provenance of its reference.

\keywords{Fairness \and Segmentation \and Spine MRI \and Bias \and Label noise.}
\end{abstract}
