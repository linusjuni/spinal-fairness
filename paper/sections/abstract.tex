\begin{abstract}

Automated cervical-spine segmentation increasingly guides clinical decisions, yet
no controlled fairness audit exists for this anatomy. When datasets mix expert and
machine-generated labels, the reference used to judge fairness can itself be
biased -- a problem demonstrated in breast MRI but not yet tested in spine
imaging. We audit an nnU-Net model on the CSpineSeg dataset (1,254 exams) across
sex, race, and age, comparing verdicts obtained with expert labels against those
from a machine-generated reference. We further test whether training on
machine-generated labels amplifies demographic bias, and probe the encoder for
latent demographic signal. The model is fair across all groups: none of 63
statistical tests reach significance after correction, and all disparate impact
ratios exceed 0.96. However, replacing expert labels with machine-generated ones makes 11 age-related
tests significant despite the model remaining fair by all threshold-based
measures -- a false alarm driven by variance collapse rather than a real
disparity. Training
on machine-generated labels does not amplify bias. Sex is strongly encoded in the
learned representation yet produces no performance disparity, showing that
encoding alone does not constitute harm. Reference-label provenance is a
first-order confounder in fairness evaluation that must be reported alongside any
fairness claim.

\keywords{Fairness \and Medical segmentation \and Spine MRI \and Bias.}
\end{abstract}
