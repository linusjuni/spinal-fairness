We work with CSpineSeg, a public collection of cervical-spine MRI released by
Zhou et al.~\cite{zhou2025cspineseg}. It provides 1,255 sagittal T2-weighted exams
from 1,232 patients at Duke University. We drop a single scan -- a localizer
series with anomalous dimensions, unsuitable for the task -- leaving a working set
$\mathcal{D}_0$ of $N_0 = 1{,}254$ exams from 1,231 patients. The task we study is
semantic segmentation: labelling every voxel of a scan as background, vertebral
body, or intervertebral disc. About $40\%$ of the exams come with segmentations
drawn by expert radiologists; the rest were labelled automatically by the authors'
own model and never checked by a human.

We use the following notation throughout (\autoref{tab:notation}). We write the
cohort as a set of exams
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, Y_i, A_i, q_i)\}_{i=1}^{N},
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes an exam and $\pi(i)$ is the patient it belongs to (a handful of
patients contributed more than one exam). Each exam pairs an image $X_i$ -- a 3D
volume over a voxel grid $\Omega_i$ -- with a reference segmentation $Y_i$ that
gives every voxel one of the classes $\mathcal{L} = \{0, 1, 2\}$ (background,
vertebral body, disc). The audit covers three protected attributes,
$\mathcal{A} = \{\text{sex}, \text{race}, \text{age}\}$; for an attribute
$A \in \mathcal{A}$ we write $A_i$ for its value on exam $i$ and
$\mathcal{D}_{A=a} = \{i : A_i = a\}$ for the subgroup of exams taking value $a$.
Finally, $q_i \in \{\mathsf{g}, \mathsf{s}\}$ records whether the segmentation is
\emph{gold} (drawn by an expert) or \emph{silver} (machine-generated).

\autoref{tab:cohort} summarises the working set $\mathcal{D}_0$. The cohort leans
slightly female (55\%), is predominantly White (65\%) with a sizeable Black
minority (28\%) and only small numbers in the remaining race groups, and is
overwhelmingly non-Hispanic. Ages span the adult range (18--89, median 56), and
scans are split across two manufacturers (Siemens 63\%, GE 37\%) and two field
strengths (1.5\,T 60\%, 3.0\,T 40\%).

These group sizes decide which fairness axes we can actually test. Sex (Female vs.
Male), race (White vs. Black), and age have enough exams in each group for a
reliable statistical comparison, so we test them formally throughout. The smaller
race categories (8\% of the cohort combined) and ethnicity are too sparse for
trustworthy subgroup inference, so we report them descriptively only.
