\subsection{The CSpineSeg Cohort}

We work with CSpineSeg, a public collection of cervical-spine MRI released by
Zhou et al.~\cite{zhou2025cspineseg}. It provides 1,255 sagittal T2-weighted exams
from 1,232 patients at Duke University. We drop a single scan -- a localizer
series with anomalous dimensions, unsuitable for the task -- leaving a working set
$\mathcal{D}_0$ of $N_0 = 1{,}254$ exams from 1,231 patients. The task we study is
semantic segmentation: labelling every voxel of a scan as background, vertebral
body, or intervertebral disc (\autoref{fig:anatomy}). About $40\%$ of the exams carry expert
reference segmentations, drafted by a team of six radiologists and one researcher
without medical training and with every final mask reviewed and approved by a
radiologist; the rest were segmented automatically by the authors' own model, and
these silver masks were never reviewed by a human -- though the mid-sagittal slice of
each unannotated scan was checked for image quality.

We use the following notation throughout (\autoref{tab:notation}). We write the
cohort as a set of exams
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, Y_i, A_i, q_i)\}_{i=1}^{N},
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes an exam and $\pi(i)$ is the patient it belongs to (a handful of
patients contributed more than one exam). Each exam pairs an image $X_i$ -- a 3D
volume over a voxel grid $\Omega_i$ -- with a reference segmentation $Y_i$ that
gives every voxel one of the classes $\mathcal{L} = \{0, 1, 2\}$ (background,
vertebral body, disc). The audit covers three protected attributes,
$\mathcal{A} = \{\text{sex}, \text{race}, \text{age}\}$; for an attribute
$A \in \mathcal{A}$ we write $A_i$ for its value on exam $i$ and
$\mathcal{D}_{A=a} = \{i : A_i = a\}$ for the subgroup of exams taking value $a$.
Finally, $q_i \in \{\mathsf{g}, \mathsf{s}\}$ records whether the segmentation is
\emph{gold} (drawn by an expert) or \emph{silver} (machine-generated).

\autoref{tab:cohort} summarises the working set $\mathcal{D}_0$. The cohort leans
slightly female (55\%), is predominantly White (65\%) with a sizeable Black
minority (28\%) and only small numbers in the remaining race groups, and is
overwhelmingly non-Hispanic. Ages span the adult range (18--89, median 56), and
scans are split across two manufacturers (Siemens 63\%, GE 37\%) and two field
strengths (1.5\,T 60\%, 3.0\,T 40\%). The volumes are strongly anisotropic: in-plane
resolution is high (${\sim}0.53$\,mm) but the sagittal slices are thick
(${\sim}4$\,mm) and few, ranging from 12 to 25 per scan (mean $15.2 \pm 1.3$), so
the through-plane direction is the limiting resolution.

These group sizes decide which fairness axes we can actually test. Sex (Female vs.
Male), race (White vs. Black), and age have enough exams in each group for a
reliable statistical comparison, so we test them formally throughout. The smaller
race categories (8\% of the cohort combined) and ethnicity are too sparse for
trustworthy subgroup inference, so we report them descriptively only.

\subsection{Anatomical Confounders}
\label{sec:confounders}

Before we can read any performance gap as bias, we have to know how the groups differ in
ways that have nothing to do with the model. A score gap can come from two places that
are not unfairness: the patients' anatomy, and how their scans were acquired. Anatomy can
make one group genuinely harder to segment: cervical degeneration becomes far more common
with age~\cite{kato2012cervical}, and the bridging and merging of adjacent vertebrae it
produces blur the boundaries the model has to trace -- a pattern we observe directly in
this cohort below. Parikh et al.~\cite{parikh2025labelbias} report the breast-MRI analogue,
where younger patients' larger and more variable tumours make those cases intrinsically
harder and open a real, model-independent gap by age. Anatomy can also fool the metric,
because the overlap scores used to
evaluate segmentations (such as the Dice score) reward large structures: a fixed boundary
error costs a smaller fraction of a big
structure's score than a small one's, so bigger anatomy can score higher at the same true
quality. Acquisition
matters too: if one group were mostly scanned on particular hardware
or protocols, the scanner -- not the model -- could drive the gap. (Imaging site, a major
confounder for Parikh et al.~\cite{parikh2025whofails} on multi-centre breast MRI, is not
a concern here, since CSpineSeg comes from a single institution.) Either way, such a
difference is a confounder, not a finding. 

We start with the anatomy, measuring for every exam in the working set $\mathcal{D}_0$ the physical volume (in
mm$^3$) of the vertebral bodies (class~$1$) and the discs (class~$2$) in its reference
segmentation $Y_i$, and comparing those volumes across our three attributes $\mathcal{A}$. For each comparison we report an effect size, which quantifies the
magnitude of the between-group difference and, unlike a $p$-value, does not grow with
sample size alone. Each effect size is the standard companion of the rank-based test we
run for that comparison: the rank-biserial correlation $r_{rb}$~\cite{wendt1972rankbiserial},
derived from the Mann--Whitney $U$ test~\cite{mann1947test}, for the two-group comparisons
(sex, race), and $\varepsilon^2$~\cite{tomczak2014effectsize}, derived from the
Kruskal--Wallis $H$ test~\cite{kruskal1952ranks}, for the three age bins. We read each on the
conventional negligible / small / medium / large scale~\cite{cohen1988power,tomczak2014effectsize}.
Because $r_{rb}$ and $\varepsilon^2$ are not on the same numerical scale, we compare these
magnitude bands rather than the raw effect-size values.\footnote{Cohen's small/medium/large
bands were derived for the Pearson correlation; we apply them to the rank-biserial $r_{rb}$
following standard effect-size practice. They are approximate conventions, not hard cut-offs.}

\autoref{tab:volumes} summarises these differences. Sex is the dominant one: men's
vertebral bodies and discs are both clearly larger than women's, a large effect in each
structure. Race matters less, and only for bone -- White patients have somewhat larger
vertebral bodies than Black patients (a small effect), while their discs are the same
size. Age behaves the same way: the vertebral bodies grow and spread out with age, so
the $\mathcal{D}_{\text{age}\ge 60}$ group has the largest and most variable bodies
(\autoref{fig:volumes}), while disc volume barely moves. So across our three axes the
disc is the only structure whose size stays put for both race and age; only sex shifts
it.

A quick sanity check rules out the scanner as the cause of these differences. GE
machines produce roughly twice the voxel count of Siemens for the same anatomy (median
$69{,}689$ vs.\ $32{,}401$ voxels per vertebral-body label, a ${\sim}2.15\times$ gap),
purely from finer in-plane resolution; but once we convert to physical mm$^3$ those
volumes line up across manufacturers (VB medians $49{,}850$ vs.\ $52{,}956$ mm$^3$, a
${\sim}6\%$ gap; the disc difference is not even significant). The group
differences above are real anatomy, not a resolution artefact.

We also counted how many separate connected pieces each label breaks into, as a rough
proxy for how ``clean'' the structure is. The vertebral-body label splits into fewer
pieces in older patients -- a median of $9$ in the under-$40$s down to $7$ in the
$60+$ group (medium effect) -- while the disc piece-count stays flat across age. That
pattern is the fingerprint of degeneration: neighbouring vertebrae bridge and fuse with
osteophytes, so the segmentation merges them into single blobs. In plain terms, older
spines are anatomically more ``merged'' and harder to delineate, and that extra
difficulty lives in the vertebral bodies, not the discs -- worth keeping in mind, since
age turns out to be the axis our audit is most sensitive to later on.

Finally, we check whether any attribute is tangled up with the scanner, which could
otherwise fake a group gap. It is not: race is independent of both manufacturer and
field strength, and sex is independent of race (all Cram\'er's $V \approx 0.05$--$0.07$,
none significant). The one mild entanglement is age -- White patients are a little older
than Black patients (median $58$ vs.\ $54$ years) -- so because age tracks with
vertebral-body anatomy, we control for age whenever we compare race.

Putting it together: anatomy differs by group, strongly for sex, mildly for race
(bone only), and with age (bone only, plus the merging). A raw Dice gap could therefore
reflect structure size rather than model quality, which is exactly the confound we have
to neutralise before calling anything bias. Two choices follow from this and carry into
the rest of the paper. First, we always report vertebral bodies and discs separately,
and treat the disc as our cleanest fairness target, since its anatomy is uniform
across race and age. Second, we evaluate with a volume-aware score (the normalised Dice
score, introduced in the \nameref{sec:methodology}) rather than raw Dice. What we have measured here is
anatomy, not model behaviour -- the confounders any honest audit must account for before a
performance gap can be read as bias.
