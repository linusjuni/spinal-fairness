We work with CSpineSeg, a public collection of cervical-spine MRI released by
Zhou et al.~\cite{zhou2025cspineseg}. It contains $N_0 = 1{,}254$ sagittal
T2-weighted exams from Duke University, and the task we study is semantic
segmentation: labelling every voxel of a scan as background, vertebral body, or
intervertebral disc. About $40\%$ of the exams come with segmentations drawn by
expert radiologists; the rest were labelled automatically by the authors' own
model and never checked by a human.

We use the following notation throughout (\autoref{tab:notation}). We write the
cohort as a set of exams
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{D} = \{(X_i, Y_i, A_i, q_i)\}_{i=1}^{N},
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes an exam and $\pi(i)$ is the patient it belongs to (a handful of
patients contributed more than one exam). Each exam pairs an image $X_i$ -- a 3D
volume over a voxel grid $\Omega_i$ -- with a reference segmentation $Y_i$ that
gives every voxel one of the classes $\mathcal{L} = \{0, 1, 2\}$ (background,
vertebral body, disc). The vector $A_i$ holds the demographic attributes we audit
-- sex, race, and age -- and $q_i \in \{\mathsf{g}, \mathsf{s}\}$ records whether
the segmentation is \emph{gold} (drawn by an expert) or \emph{silver}
(machine-generated). For fairness definitions that apply to any
attribute, we use a generic protected attribute $A \in \mathcal{A}$ taking value
$a$, and write $\mathcal{D}_{A=a}$ for the subgroup of exams with that value.
